# People Management & Team Leadership
**Role:** Senior Manager of Software Engineering – JPMorgan Chase (BBAO)

---

## Q1: How do you lead, mentor, and develop a high-performing engineering team?

**Memory Trick:** Clarity → Ownership → Growth → Feedback

- **Set clear expectations** – I define role-based expectations (junior = execution, senior = ownership, lead = system-level decisions) so each engineer knows what good looks like.
- **Drive ownership culture** – I assign end-to-end ownership of services or modules. For example, in my current role, each engineer owns a microservice including its production health.
- **Invest in growth** – I run weekly 1:1s focused on career goals, not just status updates. I pair engineers with stretch assignments aligned to their growth area (e.g., a backend engineer leading a Kafka redesign).
- **Provide continuous feedback** – I give feedback in real time, not just during reviews. Both appreciation and corrective feedback are direct and specific.
- **Build leadership pipeline** – I identify high-potential engineers early and give them visibility through architecture reviews, mentoring juniors, and stakeholder presentations.

---

## Q2: How do you evaluate engineering team performance?

**Memory Trick:** Delivery → Quality → Behavior → Impact

- **Delivery reliability** – Commitment vs actual, sprint predictability, ability to handle unplanned work.
- **Quality** – Defect rate, production stability, code review quality, test coverage trends.
- **Behavior & collaboration** – Ownership, initiative, knowledge sharing, mentoring others.
- **Business impact** – Customer-facing outcomes, cost savings, performance improvements they delivered.
- **Calibration** – I use a balanced scorecard combining quantitative metrics with qualitative peer feedback to avoid over-indexing on a single number like velocity.

> Strong line: *"I evaluate outcomes and behavior together — a high-output engineer who blocks others is not a high performer."*

---

## Q3: How do you handle a low-performing engineer?

**Memory Trick:** Diagnose → Feedback → Support → Track → Decide

- **Diagnose root cause** – Is it skill, clarity, motivation, or personal? I don't assume — I have an honest 1:1.
- **Give clear, specific feedback** – I share exactly what's missing with examples (e.g., "your last 3 PRs had production bugs we caught in QA").
- **Build a structured improvement plan** – Concrete goals, weekly check-ins, pairing with a senior engineer if it's a skill gap.
- **Track progress objectively** – 30/60/90 day milestones with measurable outcomes.
- **Decide fairly** – If genuine effort and improvement is shown, I support the recovery. If not, I take the formal performance step. Fairness to the team matters as much as fairness to the individual.

## Q4: How do you motivate and retain top talent?

**Memory Trick:** Purpose → Ownership → Recognition → Growth

- **Connect work to impact** – Engineers stay engaged when they see how their service powers a business outcome (e.g., account origination journey directly affecting customer onboarding).
- **Give them ownership** – Top performers want autonomy. I delegate decisions, not just tasks.
- **Recognize publicly** – Shout-outs in town halls, stakeholder emails copying their leadership, awards.
- **Create growth paths** – Both technical (architecture exposure) and managerial (tech lead → manager) tracks.
- **Remove friction** – I actively remove blockers — slow approvals, unclear requirements, tooling gaps — so they can do their best work.

---

## Q5: How do you onboard new engineers and ramp them up quickly?

**Memory Trick:** Orient → Pair → Deliver → Own

- **Week 1 – Orient** – System overview, codebase walkthrough, environment setup, key stakeholders introduced.
- **Week 2-4 – Pair** – Pair with a buddy on small bug fixes and feature stories to build context.
- **Month 2 – Deliver** – Own a small end-to-end feature so they touch the full stack — code, review, deploy, monitor.
- **Month 3 – Own** – Take ownership of a module or component including production support rotation.
- **Documentation** – I maintain an onboarding guide with architecture diagrams, runbooks, and FAQs so onboarding is repeatable, not tribal.

## Q6: Tell me about a time you had to give difficult feedback.

**Memory Trick:** Situation → Direct → Specific → Outcome

- **Situation** – I had a senior engineer who was technically strong but consistently dismissive in code reviews, which was hurting team morale and slowing PR cycles.
- **Approach** – I scheduled a focused 1:1, not a casual hallway chat. I gave the feedback directly with two specific examples from the past month.
- **Made it about impact** – I framed it not as "you're rude" but as "your tone is causing junior engineers to avoid your reviews, which slows the team."
- **Created an action plan** – Agreed on specific behaviors (lead with the why, suggest don't dictate) and offered to help by reviewing his comments for a week.
- **Outcome** – Within a month, his review feedback became a model for the team. The lesson for me: difficult feedback delivered early and specifically is far kinder than letting issues compound.

## Q7: How do you handle conflicts within your team?

**Memory Trick:** Listen → Separate → Align → Decide

- **Listen to all sides individually** – I get each person's perspective separately first to understand the real issue, which is often not what's stated.
- **Separate people from the problem** – Most conflicts are about technical direction or unclear ownership, not personal. I reframe accordingly.
- **Align on shared goals** – I bring it back to team and business objectives so the conversation moves from "I'm right" to "what's right."
- **Make a decision and own it** – If consensus isn't reached, I make the call with clear reasoning. Lingering conflicts hurt more than imperfect decisions.
- **Follow up** – I check in with both sides a week later to make sure the resolution stuck.

## Q8: How do you balance hands-on technical involvement with management responsibilities?

**Memory Trick:** Direction over Implementation

- **Stay deep, not wide, technically** – I stay involved in architecture reviews, design decisions, and production incidents — not in writing day-to-day code.
- **Spend time where leverage is highest** – 1:1s, design reviews, stakeholder alignment, unblocking — these scale; my code does not.
- **Code reviews selectively** – I review key PRs (security, architecture-impacting) to stay grounded but don't gate normal flow.
- **Prototype when needed** – For high-risk or unclear technical decisions, I'll prototype to inform direction, then hand off.
- **Be honest about the shift** – I don't pretend to be a full-time IC. My job is to make 14 engineers more effective, not to be the 15th coder.

## Q9: How do you manage a distributed team across geographies (e.g., US + India)?

**Memory Trick:** Overlap → Async → Trust → Equal

- **Define overlap hours** – I protect a 2-3 hour daily overlap window for stand-ups, design reviews, and unblocking.
- **Default to async** – Decisions documented in writing (Confluence, JIRA, Slack threads) so no one is blocked waiting for the other timezone.
- **Build trust both ways** – Onsite-offshore is not a hierarchy. I give offshore engineers high-visibility ownership (production owner, tech lead roles) so they're not just executing tickets.
- **Equal voice in meetings** – Camera on, round-robin in design reviews, every voice heard. I push back if a meeting becomes one-sided.
- **Travel/visit when possible** – Even one in-person trip per year significantly strengthens working relationships.

---

## Q10: How do you build a culture of innovation and accountability?

**Memory Trick:** Safe → Experiment → Own → Learn

- **Psychological safety first** – Engineers innovate when they're not afraid to fail. I publicly own my own mistakes to set the tone.
- **Create space for experimentation** – Innovation days, hack weeks, dedicated 10-15% capacity for tech debt and exploration (e.g., trying GenAI tools, automation ideas).
- **Pair innovation with accountability** – Every experiment has a hypothesis, success criteria, and a clear owner. Innovation isn't a free pass.
- **Celebrate learning, not just success** – A failed experiment that taught us something is shared in retros as a win.
- **Tie to business outcomes** – I encourage ideas that improve customer experience, reduce cost, or improve developer productivity — innovation with purpose.

> Strong closing line: *"My goal is a team where engineers feel safe to try, accountable to deliver, and proud of the impact."*
