# SECTION A — TEAM MANAGEMENT

## Q: How do you manage your team?

**Memory Hook:** Clarity → Tracking → Remove Blockers

> **Core Answer**
>
> I manage teams through clarity, proactive tracking, and fast blocker removal. Every engineer should know what they own, what success looks like, and how their work connects to business goals.
>
> I track progress continuously through 1:1s, sprint signals, and delivery trends rather than waiting for deadlines to slip. My 1:1s focus more on blockers and growth than status updates.
>
> **Example**
>
> At Cerner, engineers were spread across too many parallel initiatives. I reduced active workstreams per engineer, improved focus, and delivery predictability improved significantly within six weeks.

---

## Q: What is your leadership style?

**Memory Hook:** Servant → Ownership → Accountability

> **Core Answer**
>
> My leadership style is servant leadership with strong accountability. I focus on creating clarity, removing blockers, and helping teams grow technically and professionally.
>
> At the same time, I maintain high standards around engineering quality, delivery predictability, and operational excellence.
>
> **Example**
>
> At Cerner, I simplified ownership boundaries and reduced context switching, which improved both morale and delivery consistency.

---

## Q: How do you evaluate team performance?

**Memory Hook:** Delivery → Quality → Ownership

> **Core Answer**
>
> I evaluate engineers across delivery, quality, and ownership. Delivery means reliability against commitments. Quality means operational stability and low rework. Ownership means collaboration, initiative, and proactive problem solving.
>
> I also calibrate expectations by level — execution for junior engineers, technical judgment for senior engineers, and organizational influence for leads.
>
> **Example**
>
> I use metrics as signals, not verdicts. High velocity with poor reliability is not strong performance.

---

## Q: How do you identify and grow high performers?

**Memory Hook:** Ownership → Influence → Stretch Opportunities

> **Core Answer**
>
> High performers usually show three signals: reliable execution, ownership beyond assigned work, and influence across the team.
>
> I grow them through stretch opportunities such as leading initiatives, stakeholder communication, and architecture discussions rather than simply giving them more work.
>
> **Example**
>
> Several engineers across Optum and Cerner grew into senior and lead roles after receiving end-to-end ownership opportunities.

---

## Q: How do you handle low performers?

**Memory Hook:** Diagnose → Coach → Escalate if Needed

> **Core Answer**
>
> My first step is understanding whether the problem is individual or systemic. Unclear priorities, context switching, and lack of psychological safety often create performance issues.
>
> I coach first, remove blockers, and clarify expectations. If performance still does not improve after sustained support, I move to a formal improvement plan with clear expectations and timelines.
>
> **Example**
>
> At Optum, an engineer missing commitments was overloaded across support and multiple projects. After restructuring his allocation, delivery and QA quality improved within weeks.

**Memory Hook:** Clarity → Ownership → Growth

> **Core Answer**

>
> I also make recognition specific and timely so strong contributions feel visible.
>
> **Example**
>
> Teams stay motivated when they see impact, feel trusted, and get opportunities to grow.

## Q: How do you build engineering culture?

**Memory Hook:** Ownership → Safety → Excellence

> **Core Answer**
>
> I believe engineering culture is built through daily behaviors, not slogans. I focus on ownership, psychological safety, and engineering excellence.
>
> Teams should feel comfortable raising risks early while maintaining high standards around quality and operational reliability.

> **Example**

**Memory Hook:** Meaningful Work → Growth → Trust

> **Core Answer**
>
> Strong engineers stay where they see meaningful impact, growth opportunities, and transparent leadership.
>
> I focus on giving engineers ownership, visibility, mentoring, and opportunities to solve challenging problems.
>

## Q: How do you mentor and develop team members?

**Memory Hook:** Plan → Expose → Feedback

> **Core Answer**
>
> I treat mentoring as a structured responsibility. I run development conversations separately from performance reviews and align growth opportunities with the roadmap.
>
> If someone wants architecture exposure, I involve them in design reviews. If someone wants leadership growth, I give them stakeholder visibility.
>
> **Example**
>
> Specific and timely feedback helps people improve much faster than generic praise.

## Q: How do you scale an engineering organization?

**Memory Hook:** Structure → Platform → Standards

> **Core Answer**
>
> Scaling engineering requires more than adding people. I focus on domain ownership, shared platform capabilities, and consistent engineering standards.
>
> Shared platforms prevent duplication, while standards maintain reliability and scalability across teams.
>
> **Example**
>
> I invest heavily in internal coaching because hiring alone does not scale culture.

---

## Q: A developer says they are not getting challenging work. What do you do?

**Memory Hook:** Listen → Align Interests → Give Ownership

> **Core Answer**
>
> I treat this as a growth conversation, not a performance discussion. I first understand what motivates the engineer and where they want to grow.
>
> Then I align meaningful ownership opportunities with roadmap priorities instead of simply adding more tasks.
>
> **Example**
>
> At Cerner, I gave an engineer ownership of a V3 Java/Spark POC, including design and architecture review responsibilities. He later became the technical lead for that initiative.

---

## Q: How do you handle a team with low morale and missed deadlines?

**Memory Hook:** Listen → Diagnose → Quick Wins

> **Core Answer**
>
> When joining a struggling team, I avoid acting too quickly. I first listen through 1:1s and stakeholder discussions, then diagnose the real causes.
>
> Low morale is usually a symptom of deeper issues such as unclear ownership, too much context switching, or unrealistic commitments.
>
> Once the root cause is clear, I focus on visible quick wins that rebuild trust and momentum.
>
> **Example**
>
> At Cerner, reducing parallel workstreams improved sprint velocity significantly without changing team size.

# SECTION C — STAKEHOLDER MANAGEMENT

## Q: How do you manage conflicting priorities from multiple stakeholders?

**Memory Hook:** Shared Context → Transparent Capacity → Trade-offs

> **Core Answer**
>
> Conflicting priorities usually happen because stakeholders optimize for their own domain without seeing overall capacity constraints.
>
> I bring stakeholders together, make capacity visible, and present trade-offs clearly so business leaders can make informed prioritization decisions.
>
> **Example**
>
> I prefer solving conflicts through transparency early rather than surprising stakeholders later with missed commitments.

---

## Q: Business wants a feature in 6 weeks but engineering estimates 14 weeks. What do you do?

**Memory Hook:** Validate → Options → Avoid False Commitments

> **Core Answer**
>
> I first validate both the technical estimate and the business driver behind the timeline.
>
> Then I present phased delivery options such as full scope later, reduced scope earlier, or MVP followed by phased enhancement.
>
> **Example**
>
> I avoid unrealistic commitments because they damage trust, quality, and team morale far more than honest trade-off discussions.

---

## Q: How do you influence without authority?

**Memory Hook:** Context → Alignment → Trust

> **Core Answer**
>
> Influence without authority starts with understanding the other team’s priorities before proposing solutions.
>
> I align stakeholders around shared business outcomes, risks, and trade-offs rather than relying on escalation.
>
> **Example**
>
> During a modernization initiative, I aligned platform and product teams around a phased roadmap without needing escalation.

---

## Q: How do you build trust with stakeholders?

**Memory Hook:** Transparency → Reliability → Collaboration

> **Core Answer**
>
> I build trust through clear communication, reliable execution, and involving stakeholders early in decisions.
>
> I share risks early, avoid surprises, and communicate revised plans proactively when needed.
>
> **Example**
>
> Stakeholders trust teams that communicate problems early more than teams that stay silent until deadlines slip.
