## SECTION A — TEAM MANAGEMENT

### Q: How do you manage your team?
**Memory Hook:** Clarity → Track → Engage → Intervene

> **Core Answer**
>
> I focus on four things while managing teams.
>
> First is clarity — every engineer should know exactly what they own, what success looks like, and how their work connects to the quarterly roadmap.
>
> Second is proactive tracking. I review progress continuously through sprint burndowns, 1:1s, and delivery signals. I do not wait for sprint review to discover slippage.
>
> Third is engagement. My 1:1s are not status meetings — I use them to understand blockers, career interests, and where I can help the engineer grow.
>
> Fourth is intervention. If I see dependency issues, context switching, or process bottlenecks, I take ownership of removing them quickly.
>
> **Example**
>
> At Cerner, I inherited a team where engineers were spread across too many parallel initiatives, which was causing delivery inconsistency and low morale. I reduced active workstreams to two per engineer and improved focus. Within about six weeks, delivery predictability and team velocity improved 30% without changing team size.

### Q: What is your leadership style?
**Memory Hook:** Servant Leadership → Ownership → Clarity → Growth → Accountability

> **Core Answer**
>
> I believe strong engineering culture comes from ownership, clarity, and trust. I operate with a servant leadership mindset where I remove blockers, enable decision-making, and help teams grow technically and professionally. At the same time, I maintain strong accountability around engineering quality, delivery predictability, and operational excellence.
>
> **Example**
>
> At Cerner, I inherited a team with delivery inconsistency and heavy context switching. I simplified ownership boundaries, reduced parallel initiatives, increased technical mentoring, and improved delivery predictability significantly within two quarters while also improving team morale.

### Q: How do you evaluate team performance?
**Memory Hook:** Delivery → Quality → Behavior

> **Core Answer**
>
> I evaluate team performance across three dimensions: delivery, quality, and behavior.
>
> Delivery means whether engineers consistently meet commitments, communicate risks early, and take ownership rather than waiting until deadlines slip.
>
> Quality means defect leakage, rework, production stability, and operational reliability in the areas they own.
>
> Behavior means collaboration, initiative, raising issues early, and helping unblock others.
>
> I also calibrate expectations by level. For junior engineers, I focus more on execution consistency and learning agility. For senior engineers, ownership and technical judgment matter more. For architects or leads, I evaluate system-level thinking and cross-team influence.
>
> I use metrics as signals, not the final verdict. Velocity alone can be misleading — a team delivering fewer but high-impact outcomes is often more effective than a team closing a large number of low-value tasks.
>
> **Example**
>
> I combine quantitative metrics with qualitative observations, because a single number rarely tells the full story.

### Q: How do you identify and grow high performers?
**Memory Hook:** Deliver → Own → Influence → Stretch → Visibility → Evidence

> **Core Answer**
>
> I identify high performers through three signals.
>
> First, consistent delivery — they reliably execute without needing constant follow-up.
>
> Second, ownership — they naturally step beyond their assigned tasks and take responsibility for outcomes, not just tickets.
>
> Third, influence — other engineers start depending on them for technical guidance or execution support.
>
> Once I identify those people, I focus on giving them stretch opportunities rather than just more work. For example, if someone wants to grow into a tech lead role, I give them end-to-end ownership of a feature — including stakeholder communication, coordination across teams, and technical decision-making.
>
> I also believe visibility matters. I rotate responsibilities like sprint demos, architecture discussions, and stakeholder presentations so strong contributors become visible beyond just their direct manager.
>
> When it comes to promotions, I prefer evidence over narrative. I build a concrete case around measurable impact — things like leading critical initiatives, mentoring engineers, improving reliability, or driving cross-team delivery.
>
> **Example**
>
> Over the years, that approach helped multiple engineers grow into senior and lead roles across both Optum and Cerner.

### Q: How do you handle low performers?
**Memory Hook:** Identify → Diagnose → System First → Improve → PIP if needed

> **Core Answer**
>
> My first question is always: is this individual or systemic? Unclear priorities, unmanaged interrupts, and missing psychological safety cause most low performance. I fix the system before coaching the individual.
>
> I look at the facts before the conversation — velocity, code review turnaround, and QA feedback over a reasonable period. Then I have a direct 1:1, not a performance review, to understand what is in the way.
>
> If the issue is context switching, dependency load, or unclear priorities, I restructure the work and remove the blocker.
>
> If coaching does not move the needle after two sprint cycles, I escalate to a formal PIP with documented expectations, specific timelines, and clear consequences. That conversation is direct and honest — not adversarial, but not vague.
>
> **Example**
>
> At Optum, I had an engineer consistently missing sprint commitments. I found that he was context-switching across production support, two features, and ad hoc requests from another team. I restructured his allocation, set one focus area per sprint, and removed the cross-team requests formally. Six weeks later, velocity improved and QA friction dropped. I have handled 2 PIPs at Optum over 6 years, and both were resolved within plan.

### Q: How do you build and motivate a high-performing team?
**Memory Hook:** Clarity → Ownership → Recognition → Growth → Connect to Impact

> **Core Answer**
>
> As a manager, I focus on four things to build a high-performing team: clarity, ownership, recognition, and growth.
>
> First is clarity — I make sure engineers understand not just what they are building, but why it matters to the business and customers. I connect sprint work to outcomes, not just tickets.
>
> Second is ownership — I involve engineers in design discussions and decision-making so they feel accountable for outcomes, not just task completion.
>
> Third is recognition — I acknowledge strong contributions publicly in sprint reviews and team channels with specific praise, not generic praise.
>
> Fourth is growth — I invest in IDPs for every engineer. I ask where they want to be in 12 months, what specific experiences they need, and I make those experiences available within our roadmap.
>
> **Example**
>
> Strong teams stay motivated when they see impact, ownership, and growth together rather than as separate ideas.

Teams should feel comfortable raising risks early while maintaining high standards around quality, resiliency, and operational excellence.

### Q: What is your strongest approach when building a team from scratch?
**Memory Hook:** Skill Matrix → Structured Assessment → Onboard for Retention

> **Core Answer**
>
> When I build a team from scratch, I focus on three areas.
>
> First is the skill matrix before requisitions. I map the gap first — backend engineers, data engineers, QA automation, DevSecOps depth — before opening roles.
>
> Second is structured assessment. For IC roles, I focus on coding and problem-solving. For senior engineers, I evaluate system design and ownership. For leads, I add leadership and collaboration scenarios.
>
> Third is onboarding for retention. Most attrition happens in the first 90 days, so I assign a buddy, set 30-60-90 day goals, and run weekly 1:1s from day one.
>
> **Example**
>
> I also serve as Bar Raiser at Cerner to help raise hiring quality across teams, not just within my own org.

> **Core Answer**

### Q: How do you mentor and develop team members?
**Memory Hook:** Guide → Plan → Expose → Feedback

> **Core Answer**
>
> Mentoring is structured, not informal.
>
> I run quarterly development conversations with every engineer — separate from performance reviews. We discuss where they want to be in 12 to 18 months and what specific skills or experiences will get them there.
>
> Then I find those experiences within our roadmap. If someone wants to grow into architecture, I involve them in design reviews, not just implementation. If someone wants to present better, I put them in front of stakeholders with preparation support, not cold.
>
> Feedback is ongoing and specific. Not “good job.” Specific feedback like, “The way you handled the scope negotiation in last week's planning showed mature judgment” is what helps people grow.
>
> **Example**
>
> The goal is to make growth visible, intentional, and repeatable.

### Q: How do you scale an engineering organization?
**Memory Hook:** Structure → Platform → Standards → Grow

> **Core Answer**
>
> When I scale an engineering organization, I focus on four areas: team structure, platform enablement, engineering standards, and people growth.
>
> Team structure means moving from functional teams to domain-based teams with clear product ownership. Each team owns a domain end-to-end, not just a layer of the stack.
>
> Platform enablement means creating a shared platform team for common capabilities like infrastructure, observability, CI/CD, and GenAI tooling.
>
> Engineering standards mean defining what is non-negotiable across all teams — security gates, logging standards, incident response process — while still allowing teams to choose their tech stack within guardrails.
>
> People growth means balancing hiring with internal upskilling. Hiring alone does not scale culture.
>
> **Example**
>
> I invest in coaching the people already on the team before opening requisitions because that is what makes scaling sustainable.

### Q: A developer says they're not getting challenging work — how do you handle it?
**Memory Hook:** Two Sprints Data → Normal 1:1 → Interest Area → System vs Capable → End-to-End Ownership

> **Core Answer**
>
> This is a growth conversation, not a performance conversation.
>
> First, I review two sprints of their data before the conversation so I understand what they have actually delivered and where they are already strong.
>
> Then I have a normal one-on-one, explicitly not framed as performance management. The goal is to understand what interests them, what would feel like growth, and whether I am missing blockers.
>
> If they show genuine capability and clear interest in something specific, I align it with the roadmap. I do not give them extra work piled on existing scope; I give them a meaningful piece of work with real ownership.
>
> **Example**
>
> At Cerner, I gave one engineer end-to-end ownership of our V3 Java/Spark POC. He owned the design, the implementation, and the architecture review with the HDI AI team. He went from feeling stuck on maintenance work to becoming the technical lead for that initiative.

### Q: How do you handle a team with low morale and missed deadlines?
**Memory Hook:** Listen 30 days → Diagnose 60 days → Quick Wins 90 days

> **Core Answer**
>
> The instinct when joining a struggling team is to act fast. That instinct is usually wrong. The right sequence is listen, diagnose, act.
>
> In the first 30 days, I listen without agenda through 1:1s and skip-level conversations so I can build a complete picture of pain points.
>
> In days 31 to 60, I diagnose root causes. Low morale is usually a symptom, not the actual problem. Common patterns are unclear ownership, excessive context switching, unmanaged tech debt, unrealistic commitments, or lack of psychological safety.
>
> In days 61 to 90, I implement 2 or 3 visible quick wins such as removing blockers, simplifying priorities, improving planning discipline, or resolving a process bottleneck.
>
> **Example**
>
> At Cerner, I took over a team where engineers were context-switching across four parallel projects. I reduced it to two per engineer. Sprint velocity improved 40% within six weeks — no personnel changes, no process overhaul.

### Q: Tell me about a time you handled pressure well
**Memory Hook:** Blast Radius → Mitigate → Communicate Cadence → Learn

> **Core Answer**

>
> I first assessed blast radius, declared SEV2, opened a war-room channel, and assigned an incident commander.

### Q: Biggest leadership failure?
**Memory Hook:** Plan for Ecosystem Readiness, Not Just Engineering Readiness

> **Core Answer**
>

> **Example**

## SECTION C — STAKEHOLDER MANAGEMENT

### Q: How do you manage conflicting priorities from multiple stakeholders?
**Memory Hook:** Shared Context → Transparent Capacity → Facilitate, Don't Referee

> **Core Answer**
>
> Conflicting priorities usually signal missing shared context. Stakeholders optimize for their domain without seeing the full picture or engineering capacity.
>
> My approach is to bring the relevant stakeholders into one conversation with a priority matrix already drafted. I show engineering capacity transparently — here is what we can do this quarter, here are the options, and you rank them by business value.
>
> Engineering should never be the referee between business stakeholders. We facilitate, provide data, and surface trade-offs. The business owns the decision.
>
> I also run a monthly stakeholder sync so roadmap, capacity, and trade-offs are visible before they become problems.
>
> **Example**
>
> Surprises are the enemy of trust. If a stakeholder first hears about a delay when a deadline is missed, I have failed at communication, not just delivery.

### Q: Business wants a feature in 6 weeks, team estimates 14 weeks. What do you do?
**Memory Hook:** Validate → Three Options → Ask Why → Never False Commit

> **Core Answer**
>
> I first understand both sides before reacting. I validate the engineering estimate and the business deadline, because the real constraint may be technical, regulatory, contractual, or market-driven.
>
> Instead of saying yes or no, I present multiple delivery options with clear trade-offs.
>
> Option A is full scope, production-ready, in 14 weeks.
>
> Option B is 80% of the business value in 6 weeks, with lower-priority features deferred.
>
> Option C is an MVP in 6 weeks and the full feature in a second phase.
>
> My goal is to make the trade-offs transparent so business can make an informed decision rather than engineering simply saying no.
>
> **Example**
>
> I avoid false commitments because they damage trust, quality, and morale much more than a transparent negotiation upfront.

### Q: How do you influence senior leadership decisions?
**Memory Hook:** Context → Options → Trade-offs → Data → Align

> **Core Answer**
>
> I focus on enabling decisions, not just presenting solutions.
>
> I start with business context — what is leadership trying to achieve and what constraints are they operating under?
>
> Then I present options with explicit trade-offs around cost, risk, and timeline.
>
> I support each option with data, and I stay open to the decision going differently if leadership has context I do not.
>
> **Example**
>
> My goal is a well-informed decision, not winning the argument.

### Q: How do you influence without authority?
**Memory Hook:** Context → Data → Alignment → Trust

> **Core Answer**
>
> Influence without authority starts with understanding the other team’s priorities and constraints before proposing solutions. I prefer aligning stakeholders using business impact, risks, trade-offs, and shared organizational goals rather than relying on escalation.
>
> **Example**
>
> During a modernization initiative, platform teams wanted observability work prioritized while product teams focused on delivery commitments. I aligned stakeholders around business impact and proposed a phased roadmap that both sides accepted without escalation.

### Q: How do you handle a difficult or resistant stakeholder?
**Memory Hook:** Understand → Listen → Align → Escalate if needed

> **Core Answer**
>
> I start by understanding what is driving the resistance. Most resistance is not irrational — it usually means the stakeholder has information I am missing or feels their concerns have not been heard.
>
> I schedule a direct conversation in a one-on-one setting, ask what matters most to them, and listen without defending.
>
> Then I look for overlap between what they need and what we can deliver. Often a phased approach or a small timing concession resolves most of the resistance.
>
> If resistance persists despite good-faith effort, I escalate with both perspectives and a clear recommendation.
>
> **Example**
>
> I try to resolve resistance through clarity first and escalation only when a decision is genuinely stuck.

### Q: How do you build trust with stakeholders?
**Memory Hook:** Be Clear → Be Consistent → Be Collaborative

> **Core Answer**
>
> I build trust through transparency, reliability, and collaboration.
>
> I share bad news early, I deliver on commitments or communicate early when I cannot, and I involve stakeholders in decisions rather than presenting them with a done deal.
>
> **Example**
>
> People support what they help build.

### Q: External API is unreliable — what do you do?
**Memory Hook:** Protect → Monitor → Govern → Fallback

> **Core Answer**
>
> I use a four-layer response.
>
> First, I protect the system with retries, timeouts, and circuit breakers so the external dependency does not become my instability.
>
> Second, I monitor latency and failure patterns separately for external and internal issues so I can isolate the source quickly.
>
> Third, I govern the dependency through SLAs and escalation paths.
>
> Fourth, I design fallback strategies such as cached responses for reads, queued requests for writes, and graceful UI degradation.
>
> **Example**
>
> When our authorization dependency had transient network instability and intermittent 500s, retries, fallback handling, and dependency-level monitoring reduced customer impact significantly.

### Q: How do you work with vendors and external partners?
**Memory Hook:** Expectations → Governance → Visibility → Risk → Partnership

> **Core Answer**
>
> I treat vendor management as engineering governance, not just procurement.
>
> I set clear expectations up front for delivery, architecture standards, security controls, SLAs, escalation paths, and documentation.
>
> I run structured governance cadences such as weekly delivery reviews, architecture reviews, and dependency tracking so risks are visible early.
>
> Even when vendors do the implementation, the engineering standards remain the same.
>
> I also make sure knowledge transfer, documentation, and shadow ownership exist so we are not dependent on the vendor for operational continuity.
>
> **Example**
>
> In one modernization initiative, we standardized architecture reviews, CI/CD controls, deployment checklists, and observability requirements across all teams. Delivery predictability and operational stability improved because accountability became consistent across the program.
